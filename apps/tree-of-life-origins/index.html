CHAPTER 0What Is Life?Definition and boundary
Life is a self-maintaining system that uses energy, responds to its environment, reproduces, and has the capacity to evolve.

There is no universally accepted single definition. Scientists instead look for a combination of characteristics.

HORGASM: seven characteristics
HHomeostasis regulates internal conditions.
OOrganization uses one or more cells.
RReproduction continues a living lineage.
GGrowth builds structure through controlled processes.
AAdaptation occurs in populations over generations.
SResponse to stimuli detects and reacts to change.
MMetabolism transforms energy and matter.
Are viruses alive?

Viruses carry genetic information and evolve, but they are not cells and cannot independently maintain homeostasis, metabolize, grow, or reproduce. They use the machinery of living host cells.

Guide line: “A virus brings instructions, but the living cell supplies the factory.”
CHAPTER 1The Earliest Earth4.54–3.4 billion years ago
Formation of the Early Earth
  • Earth formed about 4.54 billion years ago.
  • The young Earth was extremely hot, with a largely molten surface.
  • As the planet cooled, crust and oceans formed.
  • The atmosphere was rich in carbon dioxide, nitrogen, water vapor, and other volcanic gases.
  • There was virtually no free atmospheric oxygen.
Guide discussion: Without oxygen, early life used metabolisms very different from the oxygen-dependent metabolism familiar to us today.
The Earliest Evidence of Life
About 3.8–3.7 billion years ago

Possible Greenland evidence

Chemical and structural evidence has been interpreted as biological, but some claims remain debated.

About 3.5–3.4 billion years ago

Stronger microbial evidence

Ancient rocks from South Africa and Western Australia preserve evidence consistent with microbial communities, including stromatolite-like structures.

Stromatolites: visible evidence of a microbial world

Stromatolites are layered structures built as microbial communities trap sediment and precipitate minerals. Some ancient examples are among Earth’s oldest visible evidence of life. Living stromatolites still form in a few modern environments.

Life Was Microbial

For nearly two billion years, Earth was inhabited almost entirely by single-celled organisms—bacteria and archaea.

Not yet:plantsanimalsfishtreesdinosaurs

Complex visible life occupies only the most recent portion of Earth’s history.

Why Earth could support long-term evolution

Earth supplied persistent liquid water, carbon-rich chemistry, minerals, environmental gradients, and continuous energy sources. These conditions did not guarantee life, but they allowed complex chemistry and long-term evolution to continue.

CHAPTER 2From Chemistry Toward LifeHypotheses and evidence
Prebiotic Chemistry

Before biology, Earth contained chemical ingredients capable of forming more complex organic molecules.

  • Liquid water and early oceans
  • Carbon-, nitrogen-, sulfur-, and phosphorus-containing compounds
  • Mineral surfaces that could concentrate or organize reactions
  • Energy from ultraviolet light, lightning, volcanism, and hydrothermal systems
Hydrothermal Vent Hypothesis

One leading family of hypotheses places early chemical evolution near seafloor hydrothermal systems. Mineral pores could have provided compartments, catalytic surfaces, chemical gradients, hydrogen, and carbon dioxide. This remains a hypothesis rather than a confirmed birthplace of life.

RNA World? — Hypothesis

RNA can both store information and catalyze certain chemical reactions. The RNA World hypothesis proposes that RNA-like systems played a major role before the modern DNA–RNA–protein system evolved.

Not established: Scientists have not reconstructed a complete self-sustaining RNA-based organism from early-Earth conditions.
Protocells

Fatty molecules can spontaneously form membrane-like compartments. Protocells are proposed chemical systems in which a boundary, heredity, and metabolism began operating together. They were not necessarily true modern cells.

FUCA? — First Universal Common Ancestor

FUCA is a hypothetical concept for a very early ancestor—or population of primitive systems—near the transition from chemistry to biology.

  • It may not have been a single organism.
  • Its biology is unknown.
  • It may predate the fully developed genetic and cellular machinery inferred for LUCA.
  • It remains an active and highly theoretical area of research.
Many Early Lineages

The earliest living world probably contained many experimental lineages. Most became extinct and left no living descendants. LUCA was therefore not necessarily the only organism alive; it is the ancestral population through which all surviving cellular life is connected.

CHAPTER 3LUCA and the First Great BranchesUniversal inheritance
LUCA — Last Universal Common Ancestor

LUCA was not necessarily the first life, one individual, or a modern-looking cell. It represents the last ancestral population shared by all cellular organisms alive today.

Likely present

  • DNA and RNA
  • Ribosomes and protein synthesis
  • A genetic code
  • Cell membranes
  • ATP-based energy chemistry
  • Core metabolic pathways

Not yet present as eukaryotic organelles

  • Nucleus
  • Mitochondria
  • Chloroplasts
LUCA’s exact age, habitat, membrane structure, and metabolism remain debated.
Bacteria

Bacteria became one of the major cellular lineages. Their later diversity included cyanobacteria, which evolved oxygen-producing photosynthesis, and the alphaproteobacterial lineage related to the ancestor of mitochondria.

Archaea

Archaea form another major cellular lineage. Many were first recognized in extreme environments:

hot springshydrothermal ventsacidic watershypersaline waters

Archaea also live throughout ordinary soils, oceans, sediments, and animal microbiomes. Many use chemical energy rather than sunlight. Modern evidence links an Asgard-archaea-related lineage closely to the host side of eukaryotic origins.

CHAPTER 4Oxygen: Earth’s First Great PollutionPlanetary transformation
Cyanobacteria Changed the Planet

Cyanobacteria evolved oxygen-producing photosynthesis. They used sunlight, water, and carbon dioxide to build sugars and released oxygen.

Sunlight + CO₂ + H₂O → Sugars + O₂
Why oxygen was initially a pollutant

Early organisms evolved in an oxygen-free world. Oxygen is highly reactive and could damage their molecules. For many anaerobic microbes, it was toxic.

Why oxygen did not accumulate immediately

Oxygen first reacted with dissolved iron, volcanic gases, rocks, and other reduced materials. These oxygen sinks absorbed it before sustained atmospheric accumulation became possible.

The Great Oxidation Event

Beginning around 2.4 billion years ago, oxygen accumulated persistently in the atmosphere. Many anaerobic lineages declined or became restricted to oxygen-free habitats, while other organisms evolved detoxification and oxygen-using metabolism.

Oxygen levels rose in stages

The Great Oxidation Event did not immediately create modern oxygen levels. Oxygen continued changing over immense spans of time, with later oceanic and atmospheric increases helping support larger, more active multicellular organisms.

Why oxygen became an opportunity

Aerobic respiration can release large amounts of usable energy from food. Organisms able to exploit oxygen gained access to a powerful energy source.

CHAPTER 5The Rise of EukaryotesAncient partnerships
Endosymbiosis I: the origin of mitochondria

An archaea-related host lineage formed a lasting partnership with an alphaproteobacterial cell. Instead of one partner destroying the other, the relationship became permanent. The bacterial partner evolved into the mitochondrion.

Archaea-related host＋alphaproteobacterial partner→mitochondria-bearing eukaryotic lineage

The exact sequence by which the nucleus, cytoskeleton, internal membranes, and mitochondrial partnership assembled remains an active area of research.

Why mitochondria matter

Mitochondria use oxygen-dependent chemistry to generate ATP, the immediately usable energy currency of cells. This energy system helped support larger genomes, active transport, movement, and cellular complexity.

All plants have mitochondria

Plant cells perform photosynthesis in chloroplasts and cellular respiration in mitochondria. Animals have mitochondria but no chloroplasts. Plants have both.

Endosymbiosis II: the origin of chloroplasts

A mitochondria-bearing eukaryote formed a second permanent partnership, this time with a cyanobacterium. That cyanobacterium evolved into the primary chloroplast.

eukaryotic host with mitochondria＋cyanobacterium→photosynthetic eukaryote with chloroplasts

This primary event gave rise to the plastid lineage inherited by red algae, green algae, and land plants.

CHAPTER 6Algae and the Green LineageNot one single group
“Algae” are not one evolutionary branch

Algae are multiple photosynthetic eukaryotic lineages with different evolutionary histories. The word describes a lifestyle and appearance more than a single natural group.

Red algae

Red algae descend from the primary chloroplast-bearing lineage and form their own ancient branch of photosynthetic eukaryotes.

Green algae and land plants

Land plants evolved from within the green lineage. Green algae are therefore the closest algal relatives of land plants, although “green algae” themselves include several branches.

Brown algae and secondary endosymbiosis

Brown algae did not inherit chloroplasts directly from the original cyanobacterial partnership. Their ancestors engulfed a red-algal cell and retained its chloroplast—a later process called secondary endosymbiosis. This is one reason algae do not form a single branch.

From green algae to land plants

Moving onto land required a series of evolutionary innovations. They did not all appear at once or in one straight line.

green-algal ancestors→embryo protection→cuticle→stomata→vascular tissue→true roots, stems, and leaves→seeds→flowers
CHAPTER 7The Branching Tree of LifeCommon ancestry
Evolution is a tree, not a ladder

No living species is the goal or top of evolution. Every organism is a surviving twig on a branching tree. Humans, jellyfish, kelp, bacteria, and birds all have equally long evolutionary histories extending back to shared ancestors.

Animals

Animals form one branch of eukaryotes. Early animals evolved in the ocean before plants colonized land. Animals did not evolve from plants.

Fungi

Fungi form a separate eukaryotic branch and are more closely related to animals than to plants.

Plants and animals are separate branches

Plants and animals diverged from much older eukaryotic ancestors. Photosynthetic algae existed before animals, but land plants appeared after early animals were already present.

What the common ancestor tells us

When two organisms are compared, the key question is not which is “higher.” It is where their lineages last shared an ancestral population and what traits were already present at that point.

Taxonomic rank versus evolutionary divergence

Kingdom, phylum, class, and other ranks help organize organisms, but ranks are human categories. The branching point—the last common ancestor—is the scientific event the Tree of Life Explorer should emphasize.

Transition to organism comparison: “Choose any two organisms. We will trace their branches backward until they meet, then identify what their common ancestor likely possessed.”
GUIDE SUPPORTSuggested Talk Sequence1–5 minutes
  1. Begin: “What makes something alive?”
  2. Test the boundary: Use viruses as the discussion case.
  3. Set the scene: Young Earth had oceans but virtually no atmospheric oxygen.
  4. Establish scale: Life remained microbial for nearly two billion years.
  5. Create the turning point: Cyanobacteria released a toxic pollutant—oxygen.
  6. Introduce partnership: A bacterium became the mitochondrion inside an archaea-related host lineage.
  7. Add photosynthesis: A later cyanobacterium became the chloroplast.
  8. Finish with branching: Animals, fungi, plants, and algae occupy different branches.
  9. Open the Explorer: Compare two organisms and find where their histories meet.
REFERENCEScientific CautionsGuide level
  • RNA World, protocells, hydrothermal origins, and FUCA are hypotheses or theoretical frameworks.
  • LUCA was likely a population and was not necessarily the first life.
  • The exact order of mitochondrial acquisition, nucleus formation, and internal membrane evolution remains debated.
  • Brown algae acquired their plastids through secondary endosymbiosis involving a red-algal ancestor.
  • Adaptation occurs across populations and generations; individuals do not evolve because they “need” to.
  • Taxonomic ranks do not always correspond neatly to equal evolutionary depths.
