Branching Story Evolution is a family tree, not a ladder.

How did a lifeless planet become alive?

Every organism—from bacteria and eelgrass to jellyfish and humans—is part of one enormous branching family tree. This app follows the earliest part of that story while separating well-supported evidence from active research and hypothesis.

What changed, why did it matter, and which living organisms still carry that inheritance?

Transition 1

The Earliest Earth

A young planet cools, forms oceans, and develops chemistry capable of supporting life.

🌍 A Planet Forms Earth begins as a hot, changing world Well Supported ⌄

Earth formed about 4.54 billion years ago from rocky material orbiting the young Sun. Impacts, compression, and radioactive decay kept the young planet extremely hot. Its surface gradually cooled and developed a solid crust.

Water vapor condensed, and stable bodies of liquid water developed remarkably early in Earth's history. These oceans created environments where complex chemistry could continue for immense spans of time.

Museum Moment The earliest evidence of life appears relatively soon after Earth became capable of maintaining stable water and crust—but the exact sequence is still under investigation.
☁ The Early Atmosphere A world without breathable oxygen Strong Evidence ⌄

Early Earth's atmosphere was dominated by volcanic gases and contained essentially no persistent free oxygen. Carbon dioxide, nitrogen, water vapor, and smaller amounts of other gases were important components. Its precise composition changed through time and remains an active field of geochemical research.

Scientific wording improvement Rather than saying the original atmosphere was simply “carbon dioxide and nitrogen,” say that it was rich in carbon dioxide and nitrogen, with water vapor and other gases, and almost no free oxygen.

Why it mattered

The first organisms evolved anaerobically—without oxygen. Oxygen was not initially a life requirement; for many early organisms it was toxic.

🔎 The Earliest Signs of Life Ancient evidence is real—but difficult to interpret Active Research ⌄

Rocks from Greenland preserve carbon and structures dating to roughly 3.7 billion years ago that have been interpreted as evidence of life. Some claims are compelling, but very ancient rocks have been heated, compressed, and chemically altered, so scientists evaluate them cautiously.

By about 3.5–3.4 billion years ago, structural, microscopic, and chemical evidence for microbial cellular life becomes substantially stronger, including evidence from southern Africa and Western Australia.

Recommended replacement for the original note “Evidence indicates life existed by at least about 3.7 billion years ago, although the oldest examples are debated. More compelling evidence of cellular microbial life appears by approximately 3.5–3.4 billion years ago.”
🔬 Guide Deep Dive

“First sign of life” can refer to several different evidence types: carbon-isotope patterns, organic carbon, microscopic structures, stromatolites, or cellular microfossils. They do not all carry the same evidentiary weight.

🦠 A Microbial Planet For most of early history, life was microscopic Well Supported ⌄

For roughly the first two billion years after life's appearance, Earth's biosphere was dominated by single-celled microbes. There were no animals, land plants, forests, fish, or flowering plants.

This does not mean evolution was inactive. Microbes developed many metabolic pathways, transformed global chemistry, exchanged genes, and established the biochemical foundations still used by life today.

Museum Moment Complex visible life is a late development. Microbial life is not a primitive footnote—it is the foundation and majority of life's history.
Transition 2

From Chemistry to Life

The origin of life is an active research problem, not a single completed explanation.

⚗ Prebiotic Chemistry Natural chemistry produces biological building blocks Active Research ⌄

Before cells existed, naturally occurring chemical reactions produced organic compounds. Possible environments include hydrothermal systems, volcanic ponds, mineral surfaces, shorelines, ice, and wet-dry cycles.

Energy from ultraviolet radiation, geothermal heat, electrical activity, and chemical gradients could have driven increasingly complex reactions.

What scientists can demonstrate Organic building blocks can form through nonliving chemistry under several plausible conditions.
What remains unknown The exact environment, sequence, and pathway that produced the first evolving living system.
🧬 RNA World? A possible bridge between chemistry and heredity Hypothesis ⌄

The RNA World hypothesis proposes that RNA-like molecules once served both as carriers of information and as catalysts for chemical reactions. Modern RNA still performs both roles in limited ways.

RNA World is not the same as a fully established historical event. Scientists continue investigating how RNA components could have formed, replicated, and become enclosed within evolving systems.

◉ Protocells Compartments before modern cells Active Research ⌄

Certain fatty molecules spontaneously form membrane-like vesicles in water. Such compartments can concentrate chemicals and separate internal reactions from the surrounding environment.

Protocells are models for possible intermediate stages. They should not automatically be called alive unless they also show heredity, variation, and sustained evolution.

? FUCA? First Universal Common Ancestor—an uncertain concept Hypothetical ⌄

FUCA is a proposed First Universal Common Ancestor: a possible earliest ancestor from which all later life descended. It is useful for discussion, but it is much less secure than LUCA.

The earliest evolving systems may not have formed a clean, modern-style family tree. They may have been populations of primitive cells that frequently exchanged genetic material.

Why FUCA is difficult to identify

  • The earliest geological record is incomplete and heavily altered.
  • Early organisms were microscopic and soft-bodied.
  • Genes may have moved extensively among primitive lineages.
  • “First life” may have been a gradual transition rather than one organism.
Guide Language “FUCA is a useful hypothetical idea, but scientists cannot yet point to a single first organism. Early life may have involved communities of primitive cells rather than one identifiable ancestor.”
⑂ Many Early Lineages The earliest history may have looked more like a web Active Research ⌄

Multiple early lineages likely appeared, changed, exchanged genes, and disappeared. Most left no living descendants. The surviving genetic systems eventually became established strongly enough for the deeper branches of the modern Tree of Life to emerge.

◎ LUCA The Last Universal Common Ancestor Strong Evidence ⌄

LUCA was not necessarily the first life. It was the most recent ancestral population from which every organism alive today ultimately descends. Scientists infer LUCA by identifying deeply shared features present in bacteria, archaea, and eukaryotes.

LUCA probably had DNA and RNA systems, ribosomes, a shared genetic code, cellular membranes, enzymes, and ATP-based energy chemistry.
LUCA did not have A eukaryotic nucleus, mitochondria, chloroplasts, animals, fungi, or plants.
What You Inherited Your cells still use the same basic information system—DNA to RNA to protein—and the same ATP energy currency inherited through life's deepest ancestry.

● Prokaryotes A useful cell description—not one natural branch Well Supported ⌄

Bacteria and archaea are both called prokaryotes because their cells lack a nucleus and most membrane-bound organelles. However, “prokaryote” describes a cell organization; it should not imply that bacteria and archaea form one branch excluding eukaryotes.

B Bacteria Metabolically diverse planetary engineers Well Supported ⌄

Bacteria occupy nearly every environment and perform photosynthesis, decomposition, fermentation, nitrogen cycling, and many other chemical transformations. Two bacterial lineages are especially important later in this story:

  • Cyanobacteria produced oxygen through photosynthesis and later gave rise to chloroplasts.
  • Alphaproteobacteria include the bacterial lineage from which mitochondria arose.
A Archaea Ancient cells closely tied to eukaryotic origins Well Supported ⌄

Some archaea live in hot springs, hydrothermal systems, hypersaline water, or acidic environments and obtain energy through chemical pathways independent of sunlight. But archaea are not limited to extremes: they are abundant in ordinary oceans, soils, sediments, and animal microbiomes.

Scientific wording improvement Do not define archaea only as organisms that live above boiling temperature. Some are extremophiles, but many live under moderate conditions.
🔬 Guide Deep Dive
Molecular evidence places eukaryotes within or very near archaeal diversity, especially Asgard archaea. The exact identity and biology of the host that acquired the mitochondrial ancestor remain under study.
Transition 5

The Oxygen Revolution

Photosynthetic microbes release a toxic waste product that eventually transforms the planet.

☀ Cyanobacteria Microbes that changed the atmosphere Well Supported ⌄

Cyanobacteria evolved oxygen-producing photosynthesis. They used light energy to build organic molecules from carbon dioxide and water, releasing oxygen as a byproduct.

light + carbon dioxide + water → organic sugars + oxygen

Early oxygen reacted with dissolved iron, volcanic gases, reduced minerals, and other chemical sinks. Only after many sinks became less able to consume it did oxygen begin accumulating persistently in the atmosphere.

Museum Moment Oxygen was once a dangerous microbial waste product. The gas we depend on today initially poisoned many organisms adapted to an oxygen-free world.
O₂ The Great Oxidation Event Sustained atmospheric oxygen begins about 2.4 billion years ago Well Supported ⌄

A major permanent increase in atmospheric oxygen occurred approximately 2.43–2.33 billion years ago. Oxygen did not immediately rise to modern levels; the oceans and atmosphere continued changing through later oxygenation episodes.

Correction to the original note Oxygen did not first begin accumulating only one billion years ago. Sustained atmospheric oxygen began during the Great Oxidation Event around 2.4 billion years ago, followed by a long, uneven rise.

Before

  • Almost no persistent atmospheric oxygen
  • Mostly anaerobic ecosystems
  • No protective ozone layer comparable to later Earth

After

  • Oxygen becomes a major planetary chemical force
  • Aerobic metabolism expands
  • Ozone can reduce damaging ultraviolet radiation
! Oxygen: Poison and Opportunity A reactive gas creates both danger and energy Strong Evidence ⌄

Oxygen is chemically reactive. It damages cells unless organisms possess protective enzymes and repair mechanisms. Many anaerobic microbes remained restricted to oxygen-poor environments, while other lineages evolved ways to tolerate or exploit oxygen.

Oxygen-based respiration can extract far more usable energy from many organic molecules than fermentation alone, although early oxygen users and the timing of different metabolic pathways remain subjects of research.

Transition 6

The Rise of Complex Cells

Eukaryotes emerged through a deep evolutionary merger involving an archaeal-related host and a bacterium.

◈ Eukaryotes Cells with nuclei, internal organization, and mitochondria Strong Evidence ⌄

Eukaryotic cells include animals, fungi, plants, algae, and many single-celled lineages. Their origin involved major changes in cell structure, genome organization, internal membranes, and energy metabolism.

Important scientific refinement Do not say that eukaryotes simply developed “as a result of oxygen metabolism.” Oxygen availability was important, but eukaryogenesis was a complex evolutionary process. The acquisition of the mitochondrial ancestor was one of its defining events.
⚡ Mitochondria Descendants of an ancient bacterial endosymbiont Well Supported ⌄

Mitochondria descend from an alphaproteobacterium that entered a lasting symbiotic relationship with an archaeal-related host. Over evolutionary time, many genes moved to the host nucleus, and the bacterium became an organelle.

Mitochondria convert chemical energy into ATP. Many use oxygen as the final electron acceptor in respiration, although some mitochondria and related organelles function in low-oxygen or oxygen-free environments.

Evidence for bacterial ancestry

  • Mitochondria retain their own DNA.
  • They divide from preexisting mitochondria.
  • Their ribosomes and genes show bacterial ancestry.
  • They are surrounded by two membranes.
What You Inherited Nearly every cell in your body contains descendants of bacteria that entered this partnership more than a billion years ago.
🌿 Chloroplasts A second great endosymbiotic partnership Well Supported ⌄

Later, a eukaryotic cell incorporated a cyanobacterium. That symbiont became the chloroplast ancestor of red algae, green algae, and land plants. Other algal groups later acquired photosynthesis through additional endosymbiotic events involving already-photosynthetic eukaryotes.

This is why plants have both mitochondria and chloroplasts: the mitochondrial partnership came first in eukaryotic ancestry, and the chloroplast partnership occurred later in one photosynthetic lineage.

✓ Five Big Ideas The core story to remember Summary ⌄
  1. The transition from chemistry to life remains incompletely understood.
  2. LUCA was not the first life; it was the shared ancestor of all living lineages.
  3. Evolution branches rather than climbs. Modern organisms are cousins, not higher and lower rungs.
  4. Microbes transformed the planet. Cyanobacteria oxygenated Earth, and bacteria became mitochondria and chloroplasts.
  5. Life's history includes cooperation and merger. Endosymbiosis is as important as competition in the origin of complex cells.
💬 60-Second Guest Explanation A ready-to-use guide summary Guide Tool ⌄

“Earth began without life or breathable oxygen. Natural chemistry produced increasingly complex molecules, although we still do not know the exact pathway to the first cells. By at least 3.7 billion years ago, life was present, and for roughly two billion years it remained microbial. Cyanobacteria later released oxygen, transforming the planet. A partnership between an archaeal-related cell and a bacterium produced mitochondria and helped create eukaryotic cells. Another partnership produced chloroplasts. Animals, fungi, algae, plants, and humans are later branches of that ancient, interconnected history.”
