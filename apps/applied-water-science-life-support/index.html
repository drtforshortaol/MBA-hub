“Healthy water for our animals is as important as healthy air is to you and me.”

The Aquarium depends on a continuous supply of clean seawater from Monterey Bay. Water quality affects animal survival, behavior, health, exhibit clarity, and guest experience.

  • Natural seawater connects the exhibits directly to Monterey Bay.
  • Life support systems move, filter, oxygenate, monitor, and return water safely.
  • Maintenance keeps pipes, pumps, filters, valves, and sensors working reliably.

Life support systems are the Aquarium’s behind-the-scenes water infrastructure. They help maintain appropriate clarity, oxygen, temperature, salinity, chemistry, and circulation for different animals and exhibits.

  • Mechanical filtration: removes particles and debris.
  • Biological filtration: supports microbes that help process animal waste.
  • Protein skimming/foam fractionation: removes dissolved organic material.
  • Ozone/UV: may be used in some systems for water clarity and microbial control.
  • Heating/chilling: keeps exhibit water within animal-specific ranges.
  • Sensors and alarms: monitor system performance and water quality.
Related: Water Chemistry · Maintenance

Because the Aquarium uses natural seawater, marine organisms can settle and grow inside intake pipes. This is called biofouling.

  • Common growth may include barnacles, mussels, algae, biofilm, hydroids, tube worms, and other organisms.
  • Biofouling narrows pipe diameter and can reduce water flow.
  • Seasonal upwelling can increase nutrients, productivity, and settlement pressure.
Related: Pigging · Seasonal Upwelling

Pigging means sending a foam or rubber plug, called a pig, through a pipe to scrape away growth and debris.

  • At MBA, pigging helps remove barnacles, mussels, biofilm, and other growth from seawater intake pipes.
  • Pigging is done roughly every six weeks, with more attention during high-growth seasons.
  • The term “pig” comes from pipeline industries that used similar devices to clean pipes.
  • The orange foam pigs are moved by water pressure through the pipe.
Guest phrase: “Those orange cylinders are like giant pipe scrubbers. They keep natural seawater moving to the animals.”

Why don’t the pipes clog completely?

They are maintained regularly. Pigging scrubs away marine growth before it can seriously restrict flow.

Why use natural seawater?

Natural Monterey Bay seawater helps recreate local marine conditions and supports exhibits connected to the bay ecosystem.

What are the orange cylinders?

They are pipeline pigs — large foam scrubbers pushed through pipes by water pressure.

Does upwelling affect the Aquarium?

Yes. Upwelling increases nutrients and productivity in the bay, which can also increase growth in seawater pipes.

Is all water the same for every exhibit?

No. Different animals and exhibits may need different temperatures, flow, filtration, lighting, and water quality conditions.
